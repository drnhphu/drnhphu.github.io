How to make a good day: Experiments

Published by Dr. Hoang-Phu Nguyen | Category: Self-Development

What defines a "good day"? Is it accomplishing all your tasks, feeling emotionally stable, or simply having enough energy left at the end of the day? Through various behavioral experiments, researchers have identified key factors that contribute to our daily well-being and productivity.

The Morning Routine Experiment

Studies suggest that how we spend the first 60 minutes of our day heavily influences our cognitive performance. Small interventions, such as delaying smartphone usage and dedicating time to mindful activities, can significantly reduce anxiety levels.

Energy Peaks and Valleys

Instead of managing time, we should focus on managing energy. Aligning demanding analytical tasks with our biological peak hours (typically mid-morning for most adults) yields better results than forcing concentration during the post-lunch dip.

By continuously experimenting with our routines and systematically recording the outcomes, we can engineer a day that is both highly productive and personally fulfilling.
